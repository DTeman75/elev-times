How times are defined & assumptions

Every trip is measured on the elevator's cycle clock, starting when the lobby doors open. Lobby→Floor = from the lobby doors opening (you board) until the doors open at your floor — the initial lobby dwell is included. Floor→Lobby = from the doors opening at your floor (you board) until the doors next open at the lobby. For each floor these two legs add up to exactly one full cycle, so no single elevator trip exceeds the cycle. Express legs make no intermediate stops; the elevator always pauses at the lobby and top. The “with stairs” view replaces the raw ride time with the effective time — the lesser of waiting for the elevator or taking the stairs — where stair effort grows as F1.3 going up and is cheap going down (see Notes & references).

Settings & scenario

Summary

Objective. Compare the automatic cycles a Shabbat elevator can run in a multi-storey building and ask which is beneficial for most people.

Conclusion. Under the default, reasonable assumptions, an express ascent (express up, stop every floor down) is the most beneficial cycle: it speeds travel for most residents, especially under the conditions where speed matters most — getting home quickly to attend to an urgent need. Its slower descent falls on the easier trip: leaving is rarely time-pressured (you go when you're ready), and many residents can comfortably walk down a few flights instead. The margin of benefit widens the more one values a quick return home. The mirror-image “stop every floor up” cycle is worst, and odd/even schemes are middling but unfair by parity — half the floors get a fast ascent and half the long round-the-top detour.

Method. The elevator schedule is reconstructed from a real building's measured timings — about 30 s for the nonstop express from lobby to top, about 6 s per floor when it stops at each, and 70 / 45 / 30 s dwells at the lobby, top and each intermediate floor (a full cycle of ≈ 10:19) — and from it we compute, for five representative floors, how long it takes to ride home (lobby→floor) and to leave home (floor→lobby) under each cycle. Two layers turn raw times into the challenges experienced. The empirical layer recognises that stairs are a real alternative only for some, depending on the number of flights: following published Borg rating-of-perceived-exertion data, the effort of climbing F flights is modelled as ∝ F1.3, and a resident's effective time is the lesser of waiting or walking — so low floors are largely insulated from a poor cycle, while upper floors, for whom climbing is effectively impossible, absorb a slow ascent in full; descending demands less exertion and is therefore escapable for nearly everyone. The subjective layer adds an urgency asymmetry: a slow ascent to get back home strands you in a lobby or elevator when you can't wait to get to a bathroom or to rest, while a slow descent to exit is experienced only when one is ready to leave — so a minute saved going up is weighted more than a minute going down (default 3:1, adjustable). Sources. Travel and dwell times are measured on the building; the climbing-difficulty exponent and the urgency rationale are documented with citations in the Notes & references at the foot of the page.

Notes & references

A. The climbing-difficulty model (F1.3)

The “with stairs” effective-time model treats climbing as disproportionately costly: the effort of climbing F flights is modelled as D = c · F1.3 (a power law; an exponential c · e0.25·(F−1) is also selectable). Descending is treated as cheap and roughly linear. This note documents the basis.

1. Why not linear

The mechanical work of a climb is almost perfectly linear in height — for a 75 kg person each floor (~3 m) is mgh ≈ 2.2 kJ, so 14 floors ≈ 31 kJ. But perceived difficulty rises faster than linear, because fatigue accumulates: glycogen use, ventilation, muscle-metabolite build-up and shrinking cardiovascular reserve all compound as the climb continues. The effect is sharper after age 60, when maximal aerobic capacity has fallen 20–40% versus younger adults.

2. Perceived exertion (Borg RPE)

On the Borg 6–20 rating-of-perceived-exertion scale, a typical healthy older adult reports roughly:

So flight 14 feels roughly 31× as hard as flight 1 — not because any single flight is harder, but because accumulated fatigue is far greater. An exponential alternative D = e0.25·(F−1) (≈ 25× at 14 flights) fits the subjective “the first few are easy, then it gets much harder” description equally well.

Caveat

No published paper establishes this exact equation for stair climbing in adults aged 60–70. The exponent (≈ 1.2–1.4) is consistent with fatigue relationships seen in prolonged exercise and with the non-linear Borg-RPE increases documented in the literature. For modelling the choice of elevator vs. stairs from observed behaviour (rather than the difficulty of the climb itself), a logistic regression of stair use would be more appropriate than perceived exertion alone.

B. Ascent vs descent — the urgency weighting

The “Overall” view weights a minute saved going up more heavily than a minute saved going down. The reasoning is subjective but consistent: a slow ascent and a slow descent are not equally costly.

Consider an ordinary, non-emergency personal need — say, needing a bathroom:

  • Returning home (lobby → your floor): the wait is spent outside your apartment with no relief available, and on a high floor the stairs are not a realistic option — so getting up quickly is genuinely valuable.
  • Leaving (your floor → lobby): you can simply catch a later elevator and wait in the comfort of your own home — your couch, your own bathroom — until it comes. The delay is an inconvenience you control, not a predicament.

So the disutility of waiting is asymmetric — high going up (you're stranded outside), low going down (you're already home). The asymmetry is sharpest for upper floors, where the stair fallback is unavailable; for low floors the whole trade-off is milder because the stairs are viable either way.

The ascent-urgency slider sets how strongly to apply this (default: a minute up is worth about three minutes down). It is a stated preference, not a measurement — set it to 1:1 to value both directions equally. A genuine medical emergency is out of scope: in that case one should use the regular, non-Shabbat elevator.
